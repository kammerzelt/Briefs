Supply Risk Source: AFIA / IFEEDER November 2025 · Verified
The vitamin supply chain you've been warned about for years is now an operational problem — not a future scenario.
The November 2025 IFEEDER study confirmed what the industry suspected: 78% of US vitamin imports come from China. For specific vitamins — biotin, vitamins B1, B3, B7, B12, D3, and K — there is no meaningful domestic production capacity in the United States at all. China also controls 62% of global amino acid production, including 91% of threonine and 84% of valine. The 90-day US-China tariff pause announced May 12, 2026 buys time, but it doesn't resolve the underlying concentration. When that pause ends, the procurement exposure is the same as it was before. AFIA CEO Constance Cullman put it plainly at IPPE: "It doesn't matter if you put a tariff in place. You have to look at what it will take for a company to decide it's wise to place a plant here."
What to do this month
Use the 90-day window to map your vitamin and amino acid exposure by ingredient, not just by category. Which vitamins in your ration are single-sourced from China? Where does your current supplier's inventory buffer sit? Which vitamins have any viable European or alternative-origin supply? That audit is the minimum response to a supply concentration of this magnitude.

Elevated Risk Source: dsm-firmenich World Mycotoxin Survey 2025 · Cargill Global Report March 2026 · Verified
Mycotoxin co-contamination is the defining feed safety story of 2025-2026 — not a single toxin spike, but multiple toxins creating compounded performance pressure.
The dsm-firmenich World Mycotoxin Survey found that 86% of North American samples tested above the recommended threshold for at least one mycotoxin. The Cargill Global Mycotoxin Report released March 2026, analyzing 389,926 samples from 41 countries, found 71% of feed samples positive and 34% above performance-based thresholds. The 2025 PROcheck survey of 199 corn grain samples shows fumonisins increased 13 percentage points and aflatoxins 4 points in the 2025 crop compared to the year prior. The defining characteristic is not a single dominant toxin — it is co-contamination. Nearly half of all samples in the Cargill report contained three or more mycotoxins simultaneously, and their effects on gut integrity, immune function, reproductive performance, and feed efficiency overlap and compound. Managing for one toxin while ignoring co-occurrence misses the actual risk the animal is experiencing.
What to do this month
If your mycotoxin testing protocol is focused on individual toxins at regulatory minimums, the 2025-2026 data suggests you're measuring the wrong threshold. The performance question is not "is this contaminated?" but "what is the cumulative load across all toxins, and at what point does it affect this species' performance in measurable ways?" Cargill's species-specific performance thresholds — 17% of samples exceeded limits for calves and heifers, 27% for broilers, 26% for nursery pigs — are a more actionable framework than regulatory limits alone.

Tariff Window Source: AFIA statement May 12 2026 · Verified
The 90-day US-China tariff pause is an inventory and contract window — not a resolution. Procurement teams have a defined period to act before conditions reset.
The US and China agreed on May 12, 2026 to reduce tariffs and pause new tariff escalation for 90 days. AFIA expressed cautious support, with CEO Constance Cullman calling it "breathing room" for trade officials to work toward a longer-term agreement. The US animal food industry exported $1.27 billion to China in 2024, while importing approximately $549 million — including vitamin E, amino acids, and finished pet food. The animal food trade relationship with China matters in both directions, which is why AFIA is supportive of the pause. But the 90-day window is finite. Mills and feed manufacturers who can use this window to build safety stock on high-exposure ingredients, renegotiate supplier contracts, or qualify alternative sources are better positioned than those who treat it as resolution rather than opportunity.
What to do this month
Three questions worth answering before the pause expires: Which of your China-sourced ingredients have no qualified alternative supplier? Which have sufficient supplier inventory to buffer a 60-to-90-day disruption? And which vitamins or amino acids could you substitute or reduce in the ration without animal performance impact if supply became constrained? The answers shape a response that's proactive rather than reactive.

Rising Signal: IPPE 2026 + AFIA programming · Conference confirmed
Precision swine nutrition is appearing at every major 2026 conference — practitioner demand is clearly building across multiple venues simultaneously.
Multiple 2026 industry events including IPPE and AFIA programming featured dedicated precision swine nutrition sessions. The concept — moving from population-average formulation to individual or phase-specific nutrient delivery based on real-time performance data — is transitioning from research discussion to operational consideration for larger integrated producers. The question practitioners are increasingly asking is not whether precision nutrition is worth pursuing, but how to implement it within existing mill infrastructure and data systems.
What to watch
The implementation gap is the story the industry hasn't written yet. Research papers on precision nutrition are plentiful. Practical guides for mill managers on how to operationalize phase-specific formulation — including the data inputs, the software, and the equipment modifications — are almost entirely absent from trade press. That's the content the practitioner audience is searching for.

Rising Signal: HPAI detection data · USDA ERS April 2026
HPAI is easing — which means the flock rebuilding and biosecurity institutionalization story is just beginning, and it's Feed Strategy's lane specifically.
USDA ERS confirmed fewer new HPAI detections in Q1 2026 than Q1 2025. Egg prices, which peaked at $8.53 per dozen wholesale, have declined significantly. The recovery phase has begun — flock rebuilding is underway, egg production is projected up 7.7% in 2026. But the operational question now shifts from crisis response to permanent protocol: what biosecurity practices built during the outbreak should be institutionalized? How does flock rebuilding affect feed demand timing and formulation? What does replacement pullet nutrition look like at scale? These are feed manufacturing and nutrition questions that the veterinary and policy press aren't covering.
What to watch
The 138.7 million birds affected since 2022 represents a significant future replacement demand. Feed manufacturers serving egg layer operations should be planning now for the formulation and volume requirements of flock rebuilding — which is a different nutritional challenge than steady-state production feeding.

Emerging Signal: NGFA / AFIA 2026 priority programming · FEED Act legislative history
The FEED Act would allow feed ingredients to make production efficiency and animal health claims — closing a competitive gap with EU, South American, and Australian manufacturers.
Feed Strategy covered the FEED Act's House introduction in 2024. The legislation — which would allow feed ingredients and additives to legally claim production efficiency and animal health benefits that are currently restricted under existing regulatory frameworks — remains a stated priority for both NGFA and AFIA in 2026 with bipartisan Congressional support. US feed manufacturers are currently competing with international counterparts who operate under regulatory systems that permit those claims, creating a structural disadvantage in formulation flexibility and product positioning. A 2026 status update on where the legislation stands and what passage would mean practically for formulators is the piece the practitioner audience needs and hasn't seen.
What to watch
Formulators and additive companies should be tracking this legislation specifically because it changes the commercial case for certain additives if it passes — not just the regulatory framework but the marketing and labeling flexibility that follows. The window to understand it before it's relevant is now.
